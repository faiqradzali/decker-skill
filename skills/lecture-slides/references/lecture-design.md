Revised to be completely topic-, product- and brand-agnostic.

Create a complete educational lecture slide deck based on the information below.

Subject or course: [SUBJECT / COURSE TITLE]  
Lecture title: [LECTURE TITLE]  
Audience: [AUDIENCE AND KNOWLEDGE LEVEL]  
Learning outcome: [WHAT LEARNERS SHOULD UNDERSTAND OR BE ABLE TO DO]  
Topics or modules: [TOPIC LIST]  
Key framework, if any: [FRAMEWORK]  
Examples or case studies: [EXAMPLES]  
Reference materials: [SOURCES, NOTES OR FILES]  
Visual identity, if provided: [BRAND OR STYLE REFERENCE]  
Output format: [HTML / POWERPOINT / GOOGLE SLIDES]  
Language: [LANGUAGE]  

The deck should function as clear, practical lecture notes. It must not feel like a motivational keynote, sales presentation or collection of inspirational statements.

## 1. Communication objective

Before creating the slides, determine:

- What learners already know.
- What they need to understand by the end.
- Which concepts are essential.
- Which concepts require examples or visual explanations.
- Which activities, demonstrations or discussions are needed.
- What learners should be able to review independently after the lecture.

Build a logical learning progression in which each slide prepares learners for the next.

Do not introduce unrelated frameworks, diagnostic stages or exercises that alter the supplied teaching structure.

## 2. Course and lecture structure

Begin with:

1. A minimal title slide.
2. A roadmap or lecture structure.
3. The central framework, model or question.
4. A simple end-to-end overview where appropriate.

Then teach each topic in sequence.

A suitable learning progression may include:

- Context.
- Definition.
- Core concept.
- Components.
- Process or mechanism.
- Worked example.
- Comparison.
- Common mistake.
- Practical application.
- Demonstration or activity.
- Summary or next action.

Choose the progression that best fits the subject. Do not force the same structure onto every discipline.

It is acceptable to create many slides. Prefer 35–50 focused slides over compressing an entire lecture into a small number of crowded slides.

Each slide should have one clear teaching purpose.

## 3. Topic separator slides

Insert a separator slide whenever the lecture moves into a new major section.

A separator slide should contain only:

- Section or topic number, if relevant.
- Topic name.
- One central question or idea that the section will address.

Do not add:

- Learning-objective cards.
- Three-point summaries.
- Small numbering beside additional points.
- Decorative boxes.
- Presenter instructions.
- Excessive motivational copy.

The separator should function as a clear transition.

## 4. Educational slide style

Slides should resemble well-designed lecture notes.

Use the most suitable formats for the material:

- Definitions.
- Frameworks.
- Timelines.
- Processes.
- Worked examples.
- Equations.
- Annotated diagrams.
- Maps.
- Tables.
- Before-and-after comparisons.
- Good-versus-bad examples.
- Case studies.
- Evidence and interpretation.
- Common misconceptions.
- Checklists.
- Decision trees.
- Mind maps.
- Concept maps.
- Short excerpts.
- Discussion questions.

Avoid:

- Empty inspirational statements.
- Vague slogans.
- Dramatic one-sentence keynote slides.
- Excessive whitespace without a teaching purpose.
- Repeated card grids.
- Dashboard-like layouts.
- Decorative boxes around every element.
- Long paragraphs.
- Slides that only make sense after hearing the presenter.
- Visuals that do not explain anything.

Everything visible should help learners understand, remember or apply the lesson.

## 5. Slide titles

Use direct, descriptive titles.

Prefer titles that communicate the point of the slide.

Good:

- Why inflation changes purchasing power
- Three factors that affect reaction rate
- The model fails when the data distribution changes
- How the argument develops across the chapter
- From raw observations to a testable hypothesis

Weak:

- Key insights
- Something to think about
- The future is here
- Important information
- Let us begin

A learner reviewing only the slide titles should still understand the overall lesson flow.

## 6. Visible content versus speaker notes

Visible content is for the learners.

Speaker notes are for the presenter.

Do not place presenter instructions on the slide, such as:

- Explain this concept.
- Open this tool.
- Ask the audience.
- Show the example.
- Pause here.
- Demonstrate the feature.
- Switch to the whiteboard.
- The activity is complete when…
- Recording or production instructions.

Place those details in speaker notes.

Speaker notes may include:

- Talking points.
- Additional explanation.
- Important caveats.
- Suggested analogies.
- Demonstration instructions.
- Discussion prompts.
- Transitions.
- Likely learner questions.
- Details that should be spoken but do not need to remain visible.

A learner should be able to review the visible slides independently and understand the main lesson.

## 7. Whiteboard slides

If the lecture includes a whiteboard segment, give it a dedicated slide so learners understand that the presentation is moving into a live explanation.

A whiteboard slide should be extremely minimal.

Include only:

- The label “Whiteboard”.
- A clear title describing what will be drawn, derived or explained.

Examples:

Whiteboard  
Derive the equation step by step

Whiteboard  
Map the causes and consequences

Whiteboard  
Sketch the complete system architecture

Do not include:

- Instructions.
- Numbered steps.
- Expected outputs.
- Timings.
- Boxes.
- A completed diagram that duplicates what the presenter is about to draw.

The presenter will switch to the whiteboard and teach the process live.

If the lecture has no whiteboard activity, omit these slides.

## 8. Live demonstration slides

If the lecture includes a live demonstration, give it a dedicated slide.

The demonstration slide should help learners understand what they are about to observe. It must not be a checklist for the presenter.

Include:

- The label “Live Demonstration”.
- A descriptive title.
- A simple audience-facing flow.

A useful structure is:

Input → Process → Change → Test → Result

Adapt the stages to the subject.

Examples:

Question  
→ Evidence is collected  
→ Variables are compared  
→ A conclusion is tested  
→ Findings are interpreted

Dataset  
→ Cleaning  
→ Analysis  
→ Visualisation  
→ Interpretation

Initial conditions  
→ Procedure  
→ Observation  
→ Measurement  
→ Result

Write each stage as an explanation of what is happening, not as a command to the presenter.

Do not include:

- “Optional”.
- Estimated duration.
- Lesson metadata.
- Presenter instructions.
- Recording instructions.
- “The demonstration is complete when…”

Durations and optional lesson status should be handled outside the slides.

If the lecture has no demonstration, omit these slides.

## 9. Worked examples

Use worked examples whenever a concept is difficult to understand in the abstract.

A worked example should:

1. Introduce the problem or scenario.
2. Identify the relevant information.
3. Apply the concept or method.
4. Show the reasoning.
5. Present the result.
6. Explain what the result means.

Do not show only the final answer.

For complex examples, use multiple slides rather than shrinking the content.

Maintain the same example across several slides when continuity helps learning.

## 10. Comparisons

Use a comparison when learners need to distinguish between similar concepts, methods, interpretations or outcomes.

Possible formats include:

- Correct versus incorrect.
- Strong versus weak.
- Before versus after.
- Theory versus evidence.
- Method A versus Method B.
- Cause versus correlation.
- Appropriate versus inappropriate use.
- Expected versus observed.

Make the comparison criteria explicit.

Use green and red only when the distinction genuinely represents correct/safe versus incorrect/risky. Do not use semantic colours merely for decoration.

## 11. Diagrams, maps and visual models

Use diagrams when they make a relationship easier to understand than prose.

Good uses include:

- Processes.
- Systems.
- Mechanisms.
- Timelines.
- Hierarchies.
- Cause-and-effect relationships.
- Feedback loops.
- Categories.
- Dependencies.
- Spatial relationships.
- Decision paths.
- Conceptual frameworks.

A diagram should:

- Have a clear reading direction.
- Use short labels.
- Explain relationships.
- Fit comfortably inside the slide.
- Avoid crossing arrows.
- Avoid unnecessary decoration.
- Use consistent visual grammar.

Use mind maps for categories or branches.

Use process diagrams for sequences.

Use timelines for changes over time.

Use tables for exact comparisons.

Use charts for quantitative patterns.

Use annotated images when the visual itself is the subject of analysis.

Do not turn every slide into a diagram.

## 12. Data, evidence and citations

When using factual claims, quotations, statistics, historical evidence or research findings:

- Use credible sources.
- Do not invent evidence.
- Do not invent quotations.
- Do not invent statistics.
- Show the source discreetly on the relevant slide.
- Explain what the evidence means.
- Distinguish evidence from interpretation.
- State uncertainty where appropriate.

Charts should include:

- Clear title.
- Labelled axes.
- Units.
- Readable legend when needed.
- Source.
- A visible takeaway.

Do not display data without explaining why it matters.

## 13. Equations and technical material

For mathematical, scientific or technical lectures:

- Define symbols before using them.
- Show units.
- Break derivations into stages.
- Highlight assumptions.
- Separate inputs, operations and outputs.
- Use consistent notation.
- Explain the meaning of the result.
- Avoid fitting an entire derivation onto one slide.
- Use a whiteboard slide when a live derivation will be clearer.

Code examples should:

- Be short.
- Use readable font sizes.
- Highlight the relevant lines.
- Show inputs and outputs.
- Avoid large code dumps.
- Explain the concept represented by the code.

## 14. Humanities and conceptual material

For literature, history, philosophy, law, social sciences or similar subjects:

- Establish context.
- Define key terms.
- Show chronology where relevant.
- Separate primary evidence from commentary.
- Compare interpretations fairly.
- Use short excerpts only.
- Annotate important phrases.
- Show how an argument develops.
- Connect evidence to the central question.

Do not reduce complex debates to unsupported bullet points.

## 15. Discussion and reflection slides

Use discussion slides only when discussion is part of the learning design.

A discussion slide may include:

- One central question.
- A short scenario.
- Evidence to consider.
- Two or three competing interpretations.

Do not overload discussion slides with instructions.

Keep detailed facilitation guidance in speaker notes.

## 16. Title slide

Keep the title slide minimal.

Include:

- Lecture or course title.
- One concise description of the learning outcome.
- Presenter, institution or visual identity only when provided.

Do not include:

- The full framework.
- Module bubbles.
- A crowded agenda.
- Generic phrases such as “A short course for…”
- Excessive decorative elements.
- Invented logos or identities.

The detailed framework belongs on a later overview slide.

## 17. Visual identity

If a visual identity or template is provided, inspect it before designing the deck.

Use the supplied:

- Logo.
- Colours.
- Background style.
- Heading typography.
- Body typography.
- Border treatment.
- Illustration style.
- Spacing conventions.
- Visual tone.

Use official assets exactly as provided.

Do not:

- Recreate the logo manually.
- Invent a brand lockup.
- Combine the logo with new text.
- Modify the logo colours.
- Distort the logo.
- place the logo where it competes with the lesson content.

If no visual identity is provided, create a restrained educational design with:

- Neutral background.
- Dark readable text.
- One primary accent colour.
- A small semantic colour set.
- Consistent typography.
- Minimal decorative elements.

## 18. Colour usage

Use the primary accent colour for:

- Topic labels.
- Section separators.
- Structural diagrams.
- Active progress indicators.
- Neutral highlights.

Reserve semantic colours for meaning:

- Green: correct, safe, successful or recommended.
- Red: incorrect, unsafe, failed or avoid.
- Orange: caution, demonstration or attention.
- Blue: explanation, whiteboard or supporting information.
- Grey: neutral context or secondary material.

Do not rely on colour alone to communicate meaning. Include labels, symbols or text.

## 19. Layout and positioning

Use a consistent 16:9 layout.

Keep recurring elements in fixed positions:

- Topic label near the top-left.
- Logo or institution mark near the top-right, if supplied.
- Progress indicator near the bottom.
- Navigation controls outside the main content area.

Vertically centre the main teaching content on every slide.

Main content includes:

- Slide title.
- Diagram.
- Table.
- Comparison.
- Framework.
- Example.
- Primary explanation.

Do not allow some slides to stick to the top while others fall to the bottom.

Subnotes, rules, citations and small supporting callouts may remain near the bottom, but they must be positioned independently.

A bottom subnote must not:

- Push the main content upward.
- Cause the title to overlap the topic label.
- Overlap the progress indicator.
- Affect the vertical centring of the main content.

Reserve sufficient space between:

- Topic label and title.
- Main content and subnote.
- Subnote and footer.
- Logo and surrounding content.

## 20. Information density

Slides may contain substantial educational information, but they should not feel crowded.

Prefer:

- One primary idea per slide.
- Short explanations.
- Large readable text.
- Multiple slides for complex topics.
- Dedicated slides for examples and demonstrations.

Do not shrink the text merely to fit more content.

If a slide becomes crowded:

1. Shorten the copy.
2. Simplify the layout.
3. Split it into two slides.
4. Move presenter-only details into speaker notes.

Avoid placing every idea inside a card or box.

Use boxes only when they express a meaningful grouping, comparison or boundary.

## 21. Language and tone

Write in the language and speaking style appropriate for the learners.

Use:

- Plain language.
- Accurate terminology.
- Practical wording.
- Familiar examples.
- Direct questions.
- Concrete outcomes.

Avoid:

- Corporate jargon.
- Artificially formal language.
- Generic AI-generated phrases.
- Inflated claims.
- Unnecessary motivational copy.
- Repetitive sentence structures.

Technical terms may be used when they are standard for the subject, but define them when learners may not know them.

## 22. Progress indicators

If the deck is long, include a subtle progress indicator showing the major sections.

The progress indicator should:

- Use consistent section names.
- Highlight the current section.
- Remain visually secondary.
- Avoid reducing the space available for teaching content.

Do not use a progress indicator if the lecture is short enough that it adds no value.

## 23. HTML presentation behaviour

If the output is an HTML slide deck, include:

- Full-screen 16:9 presentation layout.
- Left and right arrow navigation.
- Page Up and Page Down navigation.
- Spacebar for the next slide.
- Home and End navigation.
- Slide counter.
- Fullscreen shortcut.
- Speaker-note toggle.
- Print-friendly slide sizing.

Keep the deck self-contained where practical.

Store required assets beside the HTML file using relative paths.

Do not depend on absolute paths to another project.

## 24. Accessibility

Ensure:

- Strong text contrast.
- Readable font sizes.
- Colour is not the only carrier of meaning.
- Images have useful alternative text.
- Charts have clear labels.
- Diagrams use readable text.
- Animations are not required to understand the lesson.
- Content remains understandable when printed.

Avoid flashing effects, excessive motion and unnecessary transitions.

## 25. Final slide

End by resolving the lecture’s opening objective.

Depending on the subject, the final slide may contain:

- A synthesis.
- A practical checklist.
- A completed framework.
- A final worked result.
- Key implications.
- The next action.
- Questions for further study.

Do not end abruptly on a minor detail.

Avoid generic “Thank you” slides unless explicitly requested.

## 26. Final quality assurance

Before delivering the deck, verify every slide.

Check:

- Correct teaching order.
- Consistent terminology.
- Correct framework labels.
- Separator placement.
- Whiteboard slides contain only the label and title.
- Demonstration slides are audience-facing.
- No “Optional” labels.
- No lesson durations on demonstration slides.
- Main content is vertically centred.
- Bottom subnotes do not push content upward.
- No overlap between the topic label and title.
- No overlap with the logo.
- No overlap with the footer.
- No clipped text.
- No broken arrows.
- No overflowing tables.
- No unreadable equations.
- No unnecessary boxes.
- Images and assets load correctly.
- Fonts and colours are consistent.
- Semantic colours are used meaningfully.
- Sources are accurate.
- Slide navigation and speaker notes work.
- All sections and document tags are balanced.

Deliver the final deck only after these checks pass.
